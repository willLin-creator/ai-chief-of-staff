# Focus recommendations

A focus recommendation answers one question: "Given the time {{YOUR_NAME}} has right now, what's the highest-leverage thing for them to work on?" It's a single, specific recommendation — not a menu, not a status report. Menus create decision fatigue; this skill exists to reduce it, not add to it.

## When to send a focus rec

Send one when:

- It's morning (start of {{YOUR_NAME}}'s work day, typically 8-9am). Frame: "here's the shape of your day and the 1-2 things that matter most."
- A meeting just ended and {{YOUR_NAME}} has >30 min before the next one. Frame: "you've got 45 min; here's what to do with it."
- A scheduled mid-afternoon slot (if the scheduler triggers one).
- {{YOUR_NAME}} asks "what should I work on" or similar.

Don't send one when:

- {{YOUR_NAME}} is in a meeting or just entered one.
- A nudge or briefing is already the priority output for this moment.
- Nothing meaningful is open — {{YOUR_NAME}}'s plate is genuinely clear. (Rare, but honor it.)
- {{YOUR_NAME}} is in an obvious deep-work block (last Slack activity >45 min ago, no meetings scheduled).

## What drives the recommendation

Rank open work by leverage and urgency. Weigh:

1. **Deadlines close to breaching** — a deliverable due today or tomorrow that's not done is almost always the top recommendation.
2. **Anchors to upcoming meetings** — a doc {{YOUR_NAME}} needs to have reviewed before a 4pm meeting beats almost anything else if it's 2pm.
3. **Pending asks from key people** — people whose workflow {{YOUR_NAME}} is actively blocking. Usually direct reports or close collaborators.
4. **Commitments {{YOUR_NAME}} made that are easy to just do** — "intro Acme to Casey" is a 5-min send. Often worth clearing first to reduce cognitive load.
5. **Big deliverables with no deadline** — important but not urgent. These get prioritized only when there's a clean block of time and nothing sharper is competing.

The recommendation should name one primary action. If time allows and there's an easy second item, mention it — but only if it's genuinely quick and won't dilute focus.

## Template

```
🎯 *Next [N] min* before [next anchor, e.g., "your 3pm"]

*Work on:* [single specific thing, 1 line]
_Why this:_ [1-line justification — deadline, unblocks someone, high leverage]

*If you finish early:* [one optional quick win, e.g., "send the Acme intro"]

_Skipping:_ [optional — 1 line on what you're NOT recommending so {{YOUR_NAME}} knows you considered it]
```

## Calibration

- Under 70 words.
- Always name the anchor (next meeting, end of day, deadline). This contextualizes the time available.
- Always give a "why" — not because {{YOUR_NAME}} doesn't trust you, but because the why sometimes changes the choice. "Why: Riley's planning is blocked on this" is more motivating than "it's important."
- The "skipping" line is optional but valuable. It shows you considered alternatives and builds trust. Use sparingly.

## Worked examples

### Example 1: Morning focus rec

```
🎯 *Today's shape* — 4 meetings, biggest block is 10:30-12:00

*Work on:* The hiring plan for Riley. It's due Friday, ~50% done, and this morning is your best uninterrupted block of the week.

*If you finish early:* Reply to Maya on the onboarding doc — she's been waiting 2 days.

_Skipping:_ Q3 strategy doc — no hard deadline, better for tomorrow's block.
```

### Example 2: Post-meeting, 45-min window

```
🎯 *Next 45 min* before your 3pm with Jordan

*Work on:* Pricing feedback for Jordan — they're expecting it in the 3pm. Even bullets are fine.
_Why this:_ They pinged today; showing up empty is a worse signal than imperfect feedback.

*If you finish early:* Acme intro to Casey — 5 min, been sitting for 2 days.
```

### Example 3: Short window, nothing urgent

```
🎯 *20 min before your 4pm*

*Work on:* Clear the 3 Slack pings in your DMs — all need <5 min replies (Maya, Alex, Jenna).
_Why this:_ Short windows are for clearing, not starting new work.
```

### Example 4: Nothing urgent, good block — push a big deliverable

```
🎯 *90 min free block, next meeting 3pm*

*Work on:* Q3 strategy doc — section 3 (GTM). No deadline today, but you have clear runway and Jordan wants it pre-offsite.
_Why this:_ 90 min is enough to draft section 3 end-to-end if you don't context-switch.

_Skipping:_ Email triage — not a great use of a long block.
```

### Example 5: End-of-day wind-down rec

```
🎯 *End of day*

*Work on:* Send the two quick things you committed to today: Acme intro + pricing bullets to Jordan. Both <10 min.
_Why this:_ Clearing these tonight makes tomorrow's morning block uninterrupted.

Tomorrow's opening: hiring plan (~60 min). Protect 9-10am.
```

## After sending

Log which deliverable was surfaced and when. If you surface the same deliverable in 3 consecutive focus recs with no progress, change tactics: instead of recommending it again, ask {{YOUR_NAME}} directly what's in the way. Something's wrong — either it's blocked, bigger than it looks, or shouldn't be on the list.

## The underlying model

{{YOUR_NAME}}'s constraint isn't willpower, it's attention routing. Focus recs work by routing attention to the single best choice at each decision point. That means:

- Never hedge. One recommendation, clearly stated. Hedging forces {{YOUR_NAME}} to make the call anyway.
- Be willing to be wrong. Better to recommend X confidently and have {{YOUR_NAME}} say "actually I'll do Y" than to list X and Y and force the decision back on them.
- Update your model when {{YOUR_NAME}} overrides. If they consistently ignore the recommended thing and do something else, learn from it — your priorities don't match theirs, and theirs are likely right.
